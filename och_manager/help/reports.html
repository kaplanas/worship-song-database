Viewing and exporting data

The "Summary" tab has a variety of tools for summarizing worship history data. It also allows you to export the tabular data so that you can do whatever further analysis you wish.

For any summary or export, start with the following two steps in the "Load worship histories" tab:

  1. Choose a date range. The default start and end dates are the first and last dates for which your congregation has data; you can modify the date range to whatever time period you're interested in.
  2. Load worship history. There are two buttons to choose from: one for just your congregation, and one for all congregations that have chosen to share their data.
    • If you load just your congregation and then export your data, you will see everything in your worship history table, including raw lines extracted from uploaded bulletins or spreadsheets.
    • If you load all congregations, the data export will not include raw bulletin lines; you can only see the songs that were entered (and only for processed records). This is a privacy measure; bulletins often contain items such as names, phone numbers, prayer requests, and other information that members may not want to be shared outside the local congregation.
    • Other congregations are excluded if they have fewer than 50 dates with data during the selected time period.

Exporting data

After you've loaded some worship histories, an "Download" button will appear. Click it to download a .csv file. This file has some basic information about congregations and songs. It also includes song IDs from this database; you can download that data as well and join it to this table to do pretty much any kind of analysis you want.

Summarizing data

All the other tabs on the "Summary" page summarize some aspect of congregations' worship histories. Tables include a csv download button, and graphs can be exported as well (click the camera icon in the top right). Some of the graphs are interactive; try clicking on a bar to get a list of specific songs below.

These summaries are intended to be fairly self-explanatory. We welcome feedback on anything that's unclear or incomplete.

This is not a competition!

We have deliberately foregrounded summaries for just your congregation. However, most of the tabs include a way to view your congregation in the context of other congregations that have chosen to share their data. We're aware of the danger that this kind of information might invite unhealthy comparisons.

Almost none of the metrics presented here are inherently "good" or "bad". For example, the number of songs your congregation sings on a given Sunday morning depends on many factors specific to your local context; the right choice for one congregation may not be the right choice for another. We strongly encourage anyone who uses this data to be prayerful about how it is used.

  • Examples of healthy ways to use this data:
    • "We want to refresh our repertoire with some new songs, but there are so many to choose from. What are some songs that a lot of other churches have adopted? Those might be a good place to start."
    • "A few of our members are concerned that we're singing too many songs on Sunday mornings. Is our practice far outside the norm?"
    • "We want to start singing 'Psalm 34 (Taste and See)', but we're struggling musically. What congregations sing this song frequently? We might contact them and ask for advice."
    • "Wow, we don't sing many songs about baptism. What songs on that topic could we add to our repertoire?"
    • "We're just about the only congregation that sings 'Go to Dark Gethsemane' regularly. I'm proud to know that this is something unique about us."
  • Examples of unhealthy ways to use this data:
    • "We're singing fewer songs than most other congregations. Let's eliminate the scripture reading from the Sunday morning worship service so we can squeeze in more songs."
    • "The songs that are getting sung the most are 'winning'. We have to keep up and make sure we're singing all the latest songs."
    • "Lord, I thank you that we are not like that other congregation that sings all the wrong kinds of songs."
